# The Saga Begins

The rain fell in sheets of liquid neon, dissolving the holographic kanji of the noodle shop below into slick, shimmering chaos on the grime-streaked window. Inside, the air was a dead-air cocktail of ozone, stale synth-ramen, and the specific metallic tang of aging neuro-circuitry. Kaito sat hunched over the opened chassis of his Synaptic Extractor Kit, the flickering power cell casting his face in a stuttering, sickly green light. His fingers, deft and stained, traced the frayed insulation of a bypass wire, a familiar ritual of coaxing life back into obsolete tech. On the desk beside the kit, a data-slate glowed with the job order: *Elara V., Account #77B-Delta. Asset: First Kiss, category sentimental-archaic. Terminal Repossession.*

He closed the extractor's case with a worn, metallic snap. The tarnished locket around his neck swung forward, its blank, memory-bleached photo a silent accusation. He ignored it, pulling on a long coat that had seen better decades.

Outside, the city was a roaring, bleeding artery of light and water. Bio-luminescent advertisements pulsed on the sides of crumbling ferrocrete towers, their promises of synthetic joy running like watercolor in the downpour. Public Amnesia Alert screens mounted on transit hubs glitched from amber warnings to stark crimson, displaying jagged maps of spreading cognitive dead zones—neighborhoods where the past was becoming a statistical error. Kaito moved through the throng of faceless citizens, his head down, just another ghost in the machine.

Elara's tenement was a concrete ribcage picked clean by time and economic gravity. The stairwell smelled of damp paper and decay. Her door was unlocked. He found her sitting in a single wooden chair in the center of a room defiantly filled with the tangible dead: shelves of paper books, their spines cracked, their pages yellowed. A glowing data-slate on a small table pulsed with the Chroma Bank’s final notice, its sterile light an intruder in this analog sanctuary. She held a dog-eared volume of poetry to her chest like a shield, her gaze fixed on him, unwavering. There was no fear in her eyes, only a profound, crystalline certainty.

Kaito set his kit on the floor. The latches unclipped with a sound that was too loud in the quiet room. He unwound the neuro-cables, their gel-slick tips gleaming. He did not look at her face as he attached the synaptic adhesion pads to her temples. Her skin was cold. The only movement was the slow, rhythmic drip of a leaking pipe in the corner, each drop a terminal beat. He powered on the extractor. The power cell flickered, stabilized. He held the main extraction syringe, a cruel instrument of chrome and glass, and pressed the needle-fine tip to the base of her skull. Her body tensed, a single, sharp intake of breath the only protest.

He engaged the plunger.

Inside the syringe’s calibrated vial, a new light bloomed—not the harsh green of his equipment, but a soft, warm, iridescent gold, swirling like captured sunset. It was done. The asset was secured. Elara’s eyes were now unfocused, the defiant light within them extinguished, leaving only a hollow vacancy. The book of poetry slid from her slack fingers and fell to the floor.

Through the window, the city screamed. The crimson Amnesia Alerts across the skyline simultaneously flashed CRITICAL CASCADE FAILURE. Entire blocks of neon went dark, not with a power outage, but with a sudden, cognitive emptiness. The ceaseless flow of traffic faltered, cars drifting aimlessly. On the street below, the river of people stopped, a thousand vacant faces turned up toward the rain, their purpose, their destinations, their very names erased in a single, silent instant.

Miles away, in a spire of sterile chrome that pierced the clouds, Director Jin watched the Mnemonic Market data on her datapad collapse. The value of every memory-asset in the city plummeted in a waterfall of red numbers, a complete systemic rout. But amidst the crash, a single data point flared on her screen, a signature of impossible density and value, glowing with the unique, golden alphanumeric of the repossessed asset. Her lips, which had forgotten how to smile, curved into a thin line of pure, surgical acquisitiveness. She saw not the end of a city, but the isolation of its most valuable commodity.

Kaito stared at the golden memory swirling in the vial, then at the spreading cognitive blackout erasing his world outside the window, a plague he had just unleashed. He was no longer a repo-man holding a debtor’s asset. He was a man holding the trigger to a bomb that had already gone off.

Therefore, with the city’s soul dying in his hands and its architect now homing in on his position, he ran, clutching the memory that was both the poison and the only possible cure.
