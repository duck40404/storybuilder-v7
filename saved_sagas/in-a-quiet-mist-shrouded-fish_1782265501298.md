# The Saga Begins

The mist over Port Sorrow was a permanent, clinging shroud, tasting of salt and ancient rot. It coiled around the dock pilings, muffling the cry of gulls into distant, spectral complaints. Dr. Aris Thorne did not notice. He was a creature of sharp angles and frantic energy, a discordant note in the town’s grey, slow dirge. Hunched over a tangle of wires and salvaged optics—his jury-rigged spectrometer—he adjusted a corroded dial with twitching fingers, his worn tweed jacket absorbing the damp.

He muttered to himself, a running commentary of quantum jargon that the mist shredded and stole. He spoke of redshift anomalies, of terahertz-band radiation with no thermal signature, a light that was impossibly bright and impossibly cold. It was a violation of known physics, a beautiful, maddening heresy that had drawn him to this forgotten edge of the world. The numbers spilling across the cracked screen of his datapad were an elegant cascade of impossibilities, a signal that repeated with the lunar precision of the tides, yet reset its foundational noise floor every single time. It was illogical. It was perfect.

A shadow fell over the device, so devoid of warmth it felt like a physical weight. Elara Vance stood there, a figure carved from the pier itself. Her oilskin raincoat, stiff and dark as wet slate, shed the mist without a sound. She did not look at him, her gaze fixed on the Aethelburg Lighthouse, a pale stone needle stitching the grey sky to the churning grey sea. The heavy brass key hanging from a leather cord at her throat was the only point of dull light on her person.

Aris launched into an explanation without preamble, his voice a torrent of manic enthusiasm. He detailed the wave-particle duality of the photons, the way they seemed to carry information that decayed and reconstituted with every sweep of the beam. He insisted this was a solvable problem, a system that could be understood, mapped, and predicted. He gestured with the datapad, its fractured screen showing a sine wave that collapsed into chaotic fractals before resolving again, a simulation of the decay he’d witnessed.

She made no move to look at his machine. Her response was a single, quiet statement. The light was not a thing to be measured. It was a covenant. Her duty was to tend to it, not to tear it apart.

He scoffed, the sound sharp against the groaning wood of the docks. He argued that ignorance was not reverence. He spoke of the energy source required, an output that dwarfed a small fission reactor but left no trace, no heat, no radiation—a scientific ghost. He accused her and the town of worshipping a malfunction, a magnificent, cosmic short-circuit. He would find its source, he proclaimed. He would get inside that tower and interface directly with the lens mechanism.

The water, which had been lapping a gentle rhythm against the pilings, suddenly went silent. For the space of a single indrawn breath, the air itself seemed to thin, tasting of ozone and static. The spectrometer’s display flooded with a blizzard of white noise, a piercing electronic shriek that lasted only a fraction of a second. Aris flinched, dropping the datapad. When he picked it up, a fresh, spiderwebbing crack radiated from the corner of the screen, a flaw that had not been there a moment before.

---

Aris’s breath hitched, a dry rasp against the sudden, profound quiet of the docks. His fingers, trembling with a frantic energy that had nowhere to discharge, traced the new fracture on the datapad’s screen. It was a perfect, crystalline spiderweb, a piece of micro-geology formed in an instant. He spoke into the damp air, his voice a low, rapid murmur, dissecting the phenomenon for an audience of one. He hypothesized a localized atmospheric plasma discharge, a focused burst of high-frequency electromagnetic radiation causing resonant stress in the gorilla glass. A logical sequence. A solvable equation.

The sound of the sea vanished, swallowed by the thickening mist that now coiled around Elara’s boots. Her oilskin coat seemed to drink the grey light, leaving her a void in the landscape. She did not look at his damaged device. Her gaze was fixed on the path leading away from the water, her voice emerging as thin and cold as sea-spray. It trespasses.

His head snapped up. He opened his mouth to launch a tirade against Bronze Age superstition, against the willful ignorance of assigning agency to a set of physical laws. He spoke instead of the power source, a frantic, desperate explanation of the raw energy required to project a beam of non-thermal photons with such intensity. It was a cascade of impossibilities that demanded an impossible heart, a quiet engine of creation humming in the tower's base, and he would not revere it. He would tear it open.

As he gestured wildly toward the pale stone needle of the lighthouse, his eyes caught on a detail near the edge of the pier. A fisherman’s net, a heavy green tangle he’d watched an old man meticulously mend that morning, lay heaped by a cleat. A long, familiar rip yawned near its cork-line, the very same tear he’d seen patched just hours before. A brief vertigo seized him, the dock seeming to lurch beneath his feet, a momentary confusion he furiously attributed to a lack of sleep.

Elara turned from him. The movement was not a retreat but a dismissal, a slow, inexorable closing of a heavy door. She began to walk, her heavy boots making no sound on the damp wood. The heavy brass key at her throat was a pendulum marking the seconds of his failure, a metronome for the quiet, unassailable rhythm of her world.

He was left standing in the sudden vacuum of her presence, the air tasting of salt and his own impotence. The jagged edge of the new crack in his datapad pressed into his palm, a sharp point of focus in the swirling grey. Her departure was not an end to their argument, but a change in its fundamental physics. His hypotheses had shattered against her certainty. A new methodology would be required. A new point of entry.
